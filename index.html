Mesh Parking RL

A tabular Q-learning agent that learns to park a car on a mesh-discretised parking lot. The lot is laid out as a grid of cells; the car has a position and a heading; and a non-holonomic car (it can't slide sideways) has to drive in, turn, and ease into a one-cell slot facing the right way. Pure Python — numpy + matplotlib, environment built from scratch, no gym.

The environment

A car on a mesh of cells

The lot is an 8×8 grid. The state is (col, row, heading) — a cell plus one of four compass headings — flattened to a single integer for the Q-table. The car is non-holonomic, so it has four actions:

action

forward / reverse

Move one cell along (or opposite) the current heading. Driving into a wall, obstacle, or the boundary bounces back and is penalised.

action

steer-left / -right

Rotate the heading 90° in place. Turning never collides — it's how the car re-aligns before a tight approach.

goal

cell + heading

Parked means the goal cell and the goal heading. Landing on the slot pointing the wrong way doesn't count.

agent

tabular Q-learning

Off-policy, α=0.2, γ=0.95, ε-greedy decaying 1.0→0.02. Just 256 states, so the value of every state can be learned exactly.

Reward shaping

The signal that makes it learnable

+100   reach the goal cell with the correct heading   → episode ends
 −20   collision (wall / obstacle / boundary)         → car bounces back
  −1   every step                                     → favour short manoeuvres
   ±   potential-based shaping  F = φ(s′) − φ(s)    → a gradient toward the goal pose

The potential φ rewards getting closer to the goal cell (negative Manhattan distance) plus a small heading-alignment bonus. Because it's a potential difference (Ng, Harada & Russell, 1999) it speeds up learning without changing which policy is optimal — it only re-routes credit, never the destination.

The WHY write-up

Why the mesh + shaping make it work

  • The mesh turns a continuous problem into a tabular one. Real parking is continuous position + heading + geometry. Snapping the car to a grid of cells and four headings collapses it to 256 discrete states — small enough for Q-learning to visit each many times and learn an exact value.
  • Discretising heading, not just position, makes alignment learnable. With only (col, row), the agent couldn't tell "on the slot, wrong way" from "parked." Folding heading into the state and the goal test is what lets it plan around the non-holonomic constraint instead of ignoring it.
  • Shaping fixes the sparse reward without lying. With only the +100 at the goal, random exploration finds the slot just 3% of the time — nothing to bootstrap from. Potential-based shaping hands out a gradient from episode one, and because it's a potential difference it can't invent a shortcut that isn't there. Success climbs ~10%→100% inside 600 episodes.
  • Collision penalties encode the walls into the values. Illegal moves aren't forbidden — the agent tries, bounces back, pays −20, and Q-learning learns to avoid those state-actions itself. The obstacle layout is absorbed into the value function, not hard-coded into the policy.
